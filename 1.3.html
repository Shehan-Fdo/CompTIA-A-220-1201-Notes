Mobile Device Networks

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 1.3

Cellular Networks

  • Mobile devices are commonly referred to as “cell” phones.
  • The term "cell" originates from the structure of cellular networks, which divide land into geographical areas or "cells."
  • Each cell is covered by an antenna using specific frequencies to provide service.
  • Users can enable or disable voice and data services independently.
    • Airplane mode disables all cellular, Wi-Fi, and Bluetooth radios.
    • It's also possible to disable only cellular data while keeping voice services active.

3G Technology

  • 3rd Generation cellular technology, introduced around 1998.
  • Significantly upgraded data connectivity, with speeds of several megabits per second.
  • This bandwidth improvement enabled new functionalities on mobile devices:
    • GPS navigation
    • Mobile television
    • Video on demand
    • Video conferencing

4G and LTE

  • 4th Generation technology, including LTE (Long Term Evolution).
  • Based on GSM (Global System for Mobile Communications) and EDGE (Enhanced Data Rates for GSM Evolution) standards.
  • Standard LTE supports download rates of up to 150 Mbit/s.
  • LTE Advanced (LTE-A) improved upon the standard, supporting download rates up to 300 Mbit/s.

5G

  • 5th Generation cellular networking, launched worldwide in 2020.
  • Offers significant performance improvements over 4G, operating at higher frequencies.
  • The standard is designed to eventually support speeds up to 10 gigabits per second.
  • Current real-world speeds often range from 100 to 900 Mbit/s.
  • Has a significant impact on IoT (Internet of Things) devices, as high bandwidth is less of a constraint.

Wi-Fi

  • Allows mobile devices to connect to a local 802.11 network.
  • Provides high-speed data access for email and internet without using cellular data.
  • Requires a connection to a local wireless access point and has limited range.
  • Can also support voice communication through Wi-Fi Calling.
    • This feature requires support from the mobile carrier.
    • It is extremely useful in areas with poor cellular coverage but available Wi-Fi.

Hotspot

  • This feature turns your phone into a personal Wi-Fi hotspot.
  • It extends your phone's cellular data connection to other devices (laptops, tablets) via Wi-Fi.
  • Functionality and availability depend on the phone and the mobile provider's plan.
  • Using the hotspot feature may incur additional charges or use data from your plan.

Subscriber Identity Module (SIM)

  • A SIM (Subscriber Identity Module) is a unique identifier for a cellular device.
  • It's often a small, removable physical card.
  • Moving the SIM card to another phone effectively transfers your phone number and service to the new device.
  • The SIM card contains:
    • SIM ID and your phone number.
    • Information about your cellular network.
    • Storage space for contacts and messages.
  • eSIM is an embedded SIM that is built directly into the device.
    • It cannot be physically removed.
    • The service information is programmed electronically.
    • Many modern devices support multiple SIMs, such as one physical SIM and one eSIM.

Bluetooth Pairing Process

  • Bluetooth pairing is the process of connecting two Bluetooth devices securely.
  • The general process is:
    1. Enable Bluetooth on both devices (e.g., phone and speaker). This is typically in Settings > Bluetooth.
    2. Set the peripheral device to discoverable mode. This might require holding a button or a specific key sequence.
    3. On your phone, select the discovered device from the list of available devices.
    4. Enter or confirm a PIN. The PIN should be the same on both devices to complete the secure connection.
    5. Test connectivity to ensure the devices are communicating properly.
  • Once paired, devices should connect automatically in the future.

GPS (Global Positioning System)

  • GPS stands for Global Positioning System, created by the U.S. Department of Defense.
  • It uses a network of over 30 satellites orbiting the Earth to provide precise navigation.
  • A device needs to have a clear line of sight to at least 4 satellites for an accurate location fix.
  • Location (longitude, latitude, altitude) is determined by calculating the timing differences of signals received from multiple satellites.
  • Used for mobile location services like maps, directions, and geotracking.
  • Location can also be determined by triangulating signals from Wi-Fi networks and cellular towers, which is useful when GPS is unavailable.
