Motherboard Buses: PCI vs. PCI Express

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 1.2

Introduction to Computer Buses

  • A computer bus is a communication pathway connecting motherboard components.
  • Functions like a "city road system," enabling data transfer between CPU, memory, and expansion slots.
  • Key roles:
    • Unifies diverse components into a single functional system.
    • Enables expandability via expansion slots (e.g., graphics cards, NICs).
  • Physical traces on the motherboard represent bus pathways.

Legacy PCI Bus

  • Peripheral Component Interconnect (PCI) (1994):
    • Uses parallel communication (multiple bits sent simultaneously).
    • Two variants:
      • 32-bit PCI: 32 parallel lines (32 bits transferred at once).
      • 64-bit PCI: 64 parallel lines (double throughput).
    • Physical characteristics:
      • Keyways (notches) align power settings for adapter cards.
      • 64-bit cards have extra connectors and a distinct keyway.
  • Installation:
    • Align card with slot keyways.
    • Press firmly (avoid excessive force).
    • Secure with a screw to the case.
  • Limitations:
    • Obsolete in modern systems (replaced by PCIe).
    • Bulky parallel design limits scalability.

PCI Express (PCIe)

  • Modern serial communication standard:
    • Replaces parallel with serial lanes (1 bit per lane at a time).
    • Lanes are bidirectional (separate paths for send/receive).
  • Nomenclature:
    • PCIe x1: 1 lane (pronounced "by one").
    • PCIe x16: 16 lanes (higher throughput).
    • Throughput scales with lane count (e.g., x4 = 4x x1 speed).
  • Physical characteristics:
    • Keyway closer to motherboard edge (vs. PCI).
    • Smaller slots (e.g., x1) save space for compact designs.
    • Retention clip on motherboard secures the card.
  • Installation/Removal:
    • Align keyway and press down.
    • Unlatch retention clip and remove case screw.
  • Advantages:
    • Higher bandwidth efficiency.
    • Scalable (add lanes as needed).
    • Backward-compatible with some PCIe versions.

Comparing PCI and PCIe

  • Communication Method:
    • PCI: Parallel (32/64 bits simultaneously).
    • PCIe: Serial (1 bit per lane, scalable).
  • Physical Slots:
    • PCI: Wider slots (32/64-bit), keyway farther from edge.
    • PCIe: Narrower slots (x1, x4, etc.), keyway closer to edge.
  • Performance:
    • PCI: Limited by parallel interference (crosstalk).
    • PCIe: Higher speeds via dedicated lanes (e.g., x16 for GPUs).
  • Use Cases:
    • PCI: Legacy systems (sound cards, old NICs).
    • PCIe: Modern components (GPUs, NVMe SSDs, Thunderbolt).

Key Takeaways

  • Buses are the "highways" of the motherboard, critical for component communication.
  • PCI (parallel) is outdated; PCIe (serial) dominates modern systems.
  • PCIe lanes offer scalability and higher bandwidth.
  • Always handle adapter cards carefully to avoid damaging motherboard traces.
  • Check keyways and slot sizes (x1, x4, etc.) for compatibility.
