Identifying Common PC Components

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 1.2

The PC Case (Chassis)

  • The PC case (or tower) houses and protects internal components. Its design impacts expandability, cooling efficiency, and aesthetics.
  • Form Factors dictate which motherboard sizes and components fit inside:
    • Full Tower: The largest option. Offers maximum space for multiple storage drives, large graphics cards, and extensive cooling (including custom liquid loops). Ideal for enthusiasts, servers, and high-end workstations.
    • Mid Tower: The most common form factor. Strikes a balance between size and expandability. Typically supports ATX and Micro-ATX motherboards. Suitable for most gaming PCs and office workstations.
    • Mini Tower: Designed for smaller spaces. Supports Micro-ATX and Mini-ITX motherboards. Limited room for expansion cards and drives. Good for basic office PCs.
    • Small Form Factor (SFF): Extremely compact, usually for Mini-ITX boards. Requires specialized parts like low-profile coolers and SFX power supplies. Ideal for HTPCs (Home Theater PCs) or space-constrained areas.

Power Supply Unit (PSU)

  • The PSU converts Alternating Current (AC) from the wall into Direct Current (DC) used by internal components.
  • Wattage: The maximum output power. Requirements depend on the components (e.g., a high-end GPU like an RTX 4080 needs a higher wattage PSU than a basic office PC).
  • Efficiency Rating (80 PLUS): Indicates how efficiently the PSU converts power. Higher efficiency means less heat and lower energy bills.
    • Ratings range from 80 PLUS Bronze (82-85% efficient) to 80 PLUS Titanium (90-94% efficient).
  • Cabling Types:
    • Non-Modular: All cables are permanently attached. Cheaper, but results in cable clutter.
    • Semi-Modular: Essential cables (Motherboard/CPU) are attached; peripheral cables are detachable.
    • Fully Modular: All cables are detachable. Allows for the best cable management and custom aesthetics.

Optical Drives (Optional)

  • Used to read/write data to discs. They connect via a SATA interface.
  • CD-ROM/CD-RW: Reads/Writes Compact Discs (lowest capacity).
  • DVD-ROM/DVD-RW: Reads/Writes Digital Versatile Discs. Higher capacity than CDs.
  • Blu-ray Drives: Highest capacity optical media. Used for HD movies and large data backups.
  • Note: Optical drives are less common in modern builds but are still useful for archival purposes or legacy media.

Exercises & Scenarios

  • Scenario 1 (HTPC Build): A user wants a compact living room PC.
    • Recommendation: SFF (Small Form Factor) case.
    • Considerations: Must select a Mini-ITX motherboard and likely an SFX power supply or low-profile CPU cooler.
  • Scenario 2 (Gaming PSU Selection): Choosing between a 650W Bronze vs. 750W Gold for a high-end build.
    • Recommendation: 80 PLUS Gold 750W.
    • Reasoning: Provides more wattage headroom for powerful components/overclocking and operates with greater energy efficiency (generating less heat).
  • Scenario 3 (Compatibility): Can an old DVD-RW drive read a new Blu-ray game disc?
    • Answer: No. DVD drives cannot read the higher data density of Blu-ray discs. (However, Blu-ray drives are backward compatible and can read DVDs).

Real-World Application

  • Help Desk Technician: Diagnosing "No Power" issues often involves identifying if the PSU fan is spinning or if motherboard LEDs are active.
  • System Integrator: Must select components that match client needs (e.g., choosing a sound-dampened case and high-efficiency PSU for a "silent workstation").
  • Network Administrator: Knowledge of form factors and power redundancy scales up to server hardware management.
