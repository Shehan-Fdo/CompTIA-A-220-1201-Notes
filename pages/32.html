Storage Devices

Volatile vs. Non-Volatile Memory

  • Volatile memory (e.g., RAM) loses data when power is off.
  • Non-volatile storage retains data without power (e.g., HDDs, SSDs, flash drives).
  • Non-volatile options include:
    • Hard Disk Drives (HDDs)
    • Solid State Drives (SSDs)
    • Flash drives (USB, SD cards, etc.)
    • Optical drives (CD/DVD/Blu-ray)

Hard Disk Drives (HDDs)

  • Magnetic storage with spinning platters and moving read-write heads.
  • Components:
    • Platters: Store data magnetically.
    • Spindle: Rotates platters (common speeds: 5,400 RPM, 7,200 RPM, 10,000 RPM, 15,000 RPM).
    • Actuator arm: Moves read-write heads across platters.
  • Latency: Faster RPM = lower latency (e.g., 15,000 RPM > 5,400 RPM).
  • Form factors:
    • 3.5-inch: Desktops.
    • 2.5-inch: Laptops/mobile devices.
  • Random access: Data accessed via address, not sequential.
  • Mechanical failure risk: Moving parts can degrade over time.

Solid State Drives (SSDs)

  • Non-volatile memory with no moving parts.
  • Advantages:
    • Speed: Faster read/write than HDDs (no mechanical latency).
    • Durability: Resistant to shock/vibration.
    • Energy efficiency: Lower power consumption.
  • Interfaces:
    • SATA: Up to 6 Gbps (limited by AHCI protocol).
    • PCIe (via adapter card): Up to 64 Gbps per lane.
    • NVMe (Non-Volatile Memory Express): Low latency, high throughput (up to 20 Gbps via M.2).
  • Form factors:
    • 2.5-inch (SATA).
    • M.2: Direct motherboard connection (supports NVMe or SATA).

SSD vs. HDD Performance

  • SSDs outperform HDDs in:
    • Boot times.
    • File transfer speeds.
    • Application loading.
  • HDDs retain niche uses:
    • Cost-effective for bulk storage.
    • Long-term archival (if not frequently accessed).

Flash Drives & Memory Cards

  • EEPROM-based (Electrically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory).
  • Characteristics:
    • Non-volatile but has limited write cycles.
    • Portable and small form factor.
    • Not ideal for backups (risk of loss/data degradation).
  • Types:
    • USB flash drives.
    • Compact Flash (CF).
    • Secure Digital (SD), miniSD, microSD.
    • XD Picture Card (older cameras).

Optical Drives

  • Uses laser technology to read/write microscopic bumps.
  • Formats:
    • CD-ROM (~700 MB).
    • DVD-ROM (~4.7 GB).
    • Blu-ray (~25–50 GB).
  • Pros/Cons:
    • Pros: Cheap, durable for archival.
    • Cons: Slow, low capacity, obsolete in modern systems.
  • Modern use: External USB optical drives for legacy media.

Interfaces & Connectors

  • SATA (Serial ATA):
    • Up to 6 Gbps (SATA III).
    • Uses AHCI protocol.
    • Form factors: 3.5-inch, 2.5-inch.
  • SAS (Serial Attached SCSI):
    • Up to 22.5 Gbps.
    • Used in enterprise storage (e.g., HDD arrays).
    • Backward-compatible with SATA physically but not electrically.
  • M.2:
    • Direct motherboard connection (no cables).
    • Supports SATA or PCIe/NVMe.
    • Keys:
      • B key: Typically SATA.
      • M key: Typically PCIe/NVMe.
      • B+M key: Universal compatibility.
  • mSATA:
    • Miniaturized SATA (predecessor to M.2).
    • Used in older laptops/ultrabooks.

Key Takeaways

  • Speed hierarchy (fastest to slowest):
    • NVMe SSD (PCIe) > SATA SSD > HDD (15K RPM) > HDD (7.2K RPM) > Optical > Flash (USB).
  • Use cases:
    • SSDs: OS, applications, speed-critical tasks.
    • HDDs: Bulk storage, backups, archives.
    • Flash: Portable data, temporary transfers.
    • Optical: Legacy media, rare archival.
  • Future trends:
    • NVMe and PCIe dominance for SSDs.
    • Declining use of HDDs in consumer devices.
    • Optical drives phased out except for niche uses.
