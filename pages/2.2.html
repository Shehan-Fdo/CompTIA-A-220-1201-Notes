Wireless Networking Technologies

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 2.2

Wireless Frequencies

  • Wireless networks operate on specific radio frequencies. The most common are 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz.
  • 2.4 GHz Frequency:
    • Pros: Longer range, better at penetrating solid objects (like walls).
    • Cons: Slower speeds, prone to interference from many household devices (microwaves, cordless phones, etc.), and has fewer non-overlapping channels.
  • 5 GHz Frequency:
    • Pros: Faster speeds, less interference due to more available channels.
    • Cons: Shorter range, less effective at penetrating solid objects.
  • 6 GHz Frequency:
    • Pros: Introduced with Wi-Fi 6E, offers the highest data rates, minimal interference, and a large number of non-overlapping channels.
    • Cons: Shortest range and requires newer, compatible devices (Wi-Fi 6E or newer).

802.11 Wi-Fi Standards

  • The IEEE 802.11 standard defines the protocols for Wi-Fi communication.
  • Common standards you should know:
    • 802.11a: 5 GHz, 54 Mbps
    • 802.11b: 2.4 GHz, 11 Mbps
    • 802.11g: 2.4 GHz, 54 Mbps
    • 802.11n (Wi-Fi 4): 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz, up to 600 Mbps
    • 802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5): 5 GHz only, over 1 Gbps
    • 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6/6E): 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz, significantly faster speeds and better performance in congested areas.

Channels and Bandwidth

  • Each frequency band (2.4, 5, 6 GHz) is divided into smaller segments called channels.
  • Routers transmit data on these channels. Using non-overlapping channels is crucial to avoid interference, especially on the crowded 2.4 GHz band.
  • Channel bandwidth (e.g., 20, 40, 80, 160 MHz) refers to the "width" of the channel. Wider channels can carry more data, resulting in faster speeds, but are more susceptible to interference.

Other Wireless Technologies

  • Bluetooth: A short-range, low-power technology used for connecting peripherals like keyboards, mice, and headsets.
    • Operates in the 2.4 GHz frequency band.
    • Uses frequency hopping to reduce interference.
    • Consumer devices typically have a range of about 10 meters (Class 2).
  • NFC (Near Field Communication): A very short-range technology (a few centimeters) used for mobile payments, data transfer, and pairing devices by tapping them together.
  • RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification): Uses radio waves to track tags attached to objects. Used for inventory management, access control, and more.
