Power Supply Fundamentals

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 2.1

Safety Warnings

  • Always disconnect from any power source before working inside a computer.
  • Risk of electrocution from both direct power sources and stored energy in capacitors.
  • Never connect yourself to a building's electrical system, including the ground wire (may be energized).
  • Double-check disconnection from the main power source before working.

Power Supply Basics

  • Converts AC (Alternating Current) from wall outlets to DC (Direct Current) for computer components.
  • Input voltages: 120V AC (US/Canada, 60Hz) or 240V AC (Europe, 50Hz).
  • Output voltages: 3.3V, 5V, and 12V DC.
  • Critical component: All internal parts rely on the power supply's output.

Electrical Terms

  • Ampere (Amp/A): Number of electrons passing a point per second (like water volume in a hose).
  • Voltage (V): Electrical pressure (like water pressure in a hose).
  • Wattage (W): Real power use, calculated as Volts × Amps (e.g., 120V × 0.5A = 60W).
  • Hertz (Hz): Frequency of AC cycles per second (e.g., 60Hz in US, 50Hz in Europe).

AC vs. DC Power

  • AC (Alternating Current): Reverses direction continuously (waveform symbol: ~).
  • Used for power transmission from plants to outlets.
  • DC (Direct Current): Flows in one direction (symbol: — with dotted line).
  • Required by motherboards and internal components.

Power Supply Input

  • Manual voltage switches on older power supplies: Set to 110–120V or 220–240V.
  • Modern power supplies: Auto-sensing (adjusts automatically).
  • Warning: Plugging a 120V-set power supply into 230V will cause catastrophic failure.
  • Use a multimeter to verify outlet voltage if unsure.

Power Supply Output

  • Outputs DC voltages with positive/negative values (relative to ground).
  • Common outputs:
    • +12V: PCIe adapters, hard drives, high-power components.
    • +5V: Older motherboard components (less common now).
    • +3.3V: Modern motherboards, M.2 slots, RAM.
    • +5V SB (Standby): Powers motherboard in sleep mode for wake-on-LAN or button press.
    • -12V: Integrated LAN, legacy PCI cards.
    • -5V: Legacy adapter cards (rarely used today).
  • Output specifications listed in the power supply manual or on the unit's label.

Motherboard Connections

  • Primary connection: 24-pin ATX connector (older systems may use 20-pin).
  • Provides 3.3V, 5V, and 12V to the motherboard.
  • Keyed design prevents incorrect insertion.
  • For 20-pin motherboards: Use the 24-pin connector but leave the last 4 pins unconnected.

Power Supply Types

  • Non-modular: Fixed cables (budget-friendly but messy).
  • Modular: Detachable cables (customizable, reduces clutter).
  • Hybrid: Mix of fixed and modular cables.
  • Redundant: Multiple power supplies in servers (hot-swappable, 50% load each, failsafe).

Power Supply Selection

  • Calculate total wattage required by all components (CPU, GPU, storage, etc.).
  • Rule of thumb: Choose a power supply rated for 50% more than your current needs for future upgrades.
  • High-end GPUs may require significant additional power (check documentation).
  • Physical size is standardized; higher wattage does not mean larger dimensions.

Efficiency Ratings

  • Measures how effectively the power supply converts AC to DC (less waste = less heat).
  • Certifications:
    • 80 Plus: 80% efficiency (basic).
    • 80 Plus Bronze/Silver/Gold/Platinum/Titanium: Increasing efficiency (up to 96%).
  • Higher efficiency = lower electricity costs and cooling needs.
