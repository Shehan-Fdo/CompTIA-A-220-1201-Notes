Motherboard Power and Connectors

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 1.2

Main Power Connector (ATX 20/24-pin)

  • Supplies 3.3V, 5V, and 12V DC power to the motherboard and components.
  • Older motherboards use a 20-pin connector; newer ones use 24-pin.
  • A 24-pin power supply can work with a 20-pin motherboard (4 pins left unconnected).
  • Modular power supplies allow removing 4 pins to match a 20-pin motherboard.
  • Features keying (asymmetrical pin shapes) to prevent incorrect insertion.
  • Includes a locking latch to secure the connection.

PCI Express Power Connectors

  • Used for components requiring additional power (e.g., GPUs, high-performance adapter cards).
  • Types:
    • 6-pin: Provides 75W of 12V power.
    • 8-pin: Provides 150W of 12V power.
  • Some 8-pin connectors can be converted to 6-pin by removing 2 pins.
  • Example: High-end graphics cards often require 6-pin or 8-pin supplementary power.

SATA and eSATA Connectors

  • SATA data connectors:
    • L-shaped 7-pin interface for data transfer to storage devices (HDDs/SSDs).
  • eSATA (External SATA):
    • Provides external SATA connectivity (e.g., via expansion cards).
    • Example: eSATA expansion card adds two external ports to a desktop.

Pin Headers

  • Groups of exposed pins on the motherboard for connecting peripherals.
  • Common uses:
    • Front panel controls (power button, reset button, LED indicators).
    • USB 2.0/3.0 ports (internal case connections).
    • TPM (Trusted Platform Module) for security.
    • Audio/speaker connections.
  • Labeling on the motherboard guides correct wiring (e.g., PWR_SW, HDD_LED).

M.2 Connectors

  • Small, high-speed slot for NVMe SSDs or Wi-Fi/Bluetooth cards.
  • Features:
    • Direct plug-and-play installation (no cables).
    • Secured with a screw to prevent dislodging.
    • Supports PCIe or SATA protocols (depends on motherboard/device).
  • Example: M.2 SSD is significantly smaller than a traditional 2.5" SATA SSD.
